Every Part of County Dublin. At Your Door.

TRASNA is a publicly owned autonomous transport system for every resident of County Dublin — from Balbriggan to Shankill, from Tallaght to Dún Laoghaire, from Blanchardstown to Ringsend. Whether you live in Swords, Clondalkin, Stillorgan, or the city centre, a TRASNA is always three minutes away — morning, noon, or midnight. No waiting in the rain. No taxi queues. No surge pricing. Just a clean, safe, autonomous car at your door.

Built to Last

TRASNA is not just a fleet of cars; it is critical national infrastructure. It is designed to be secure, reliable, and continuously improving.

Your Data is Yours

TRASNA will know where you travel, which is why privacy is written into its charter. TRASNA will never sell your personal data, location history, or travel patterns to third parties. It is a public service, not a data broker.

Self-Maintaining Fleet

A TRASNA microcar is built for a 7-year, 250,000km lifespan. Because they are electric, they have 50% lower maintenance costs than petrol cars. When a vehicle's sensors detect a maintenance need or a drop in cleanliness, it simply drives itself to a depot located strategically across the four local authority areas for servicing.

1,500 New Jobs

While the cars are driverless, the system requires a massive human workforce. TRASNA will directly employ approximately 1,500 people—including remote assistance operators, depot technicians, mechanics, cleaners, and software engineers—to keep the county moving.

Military-Grade Security

Could the system be hacked? Security is our foundational pillar. TRASNA vehicles do not rely on a live internet connection to drive safely—all collision avoidance happens on the car itself. The fleet operates on an isolated, encrypted sub-network, meaning a breach cannot spread.

Better Every Year

Like your smartphone, TRASNA improves over time. Every year, the fleet receives major Over-The-Air (OTA) software upgrades—delivering smoother cornering, better routing, and new app features. As older cars are retired, new models arrive with comfier seats and better sound systems.

Questions & Answers

Who pays for it?
The capital cost of €1.22 billion is less than one-eighth of the central estimate for MetroLink. The system generates approximately €305 million a year in subscriptions and advertising, requiring an initial Public Service Obligation (PSO) subvention of just €115 million a year — less than what the State currently pays to run Dublin Bus. As take-up increases, the system will reach break-even, and any surpluses will be reinvested in continuous improvements.
Is the technology ready?
Yes. Waymo has been operating fully autonomous taxis in San Francisco and Phoenix since 2020, completing over 2 million paid trips. Sensor costs have fallen 99% since 2015. The technology is mature, proven, and ready for deployment.
What happens to taxi drivers?
TRASNA will be phased in over three years, giving the taxi industry time to adjust. Drivers can transition to fleet management, vehicle maintenance, customer service, or other roles within the system. A €50 million transition fund will support retraining and early retirement.
Won't this just create a different kind of traffic jam?
No. TRASNA vehicles use Vehicle-to-Vehicle (V2V) communication to merge seamlessly at junctions without stopping — a "zipper merge" that eliminates traffic lights and stop-start congestion. Research shows this increases road capacity by 40% at 100% AV penetration.
How long will the cars last, and who maintains them?
Electric vehicles have far fewer moving parts than petrol cars. A TRASNA microcar is designed for a 7-year or 250,000km lifespan in heavy fleet use. Maintenance is handled centrally at dedicated TRASNA depots located strategically across the four local authority areas. Because the vehicles are autonomous, they drive themselves to the depot when their sensors detect a maintenance need or when they require deep cleaning. The system will employ approximately 1,500 people directly—including mechanics, cleaners, remote assistance operators, and software engineers.
What about software security? Could the system be hacked?
Security is the foundational pillar of the system. TRASNA operates as critical national infrastructure. The vehicles do not rely on a continuous internet connection to drive safely—all collision avoidance and navigation processing happens on the car itself. The central network only provides routing instructions. Furthermore, the system uses military-grade encryption and isolated sub-networks, meaning a theoretical breach of one vehicle cannot spread to the fleet.
Will the cars get outdated quickly?
No. Like a modern smartphone, TRASNA vehicles improve over time through Over-The-Air (OTA) software updates. Every year, the fleet receives a major system upgrade—delivering smoother cornering, better route prediction, and new app features. Hardware improvements (like comfier seats or better sound systems) are rolled out continuously as older vehicles are cycled out of the fleet and replaced with newer models, ensuring the user experience is always improving.
How much does it cost to use?
TRASNA operates on a subscription model with no per-journey fares. The pricing tiers are designed to be universally affordable:
  • €365/year: Annual adult subscription
  • €100/year: Concession for students, under-18s, pensioners, and disability benefit recipients
  • €50/month: Rolling monthly subscription for all users
  • €25/week: Rolling weekly subscription for all users
This tiered structure generates approximately €285 million in annual subscription revenue. In addition, TRASNA will generate commercial revenue through vehicle exterior sponsorship and non-intrusive in-app advertising, similar to the Dublin Bikes and Dublin Bus models. Given the fleet size and visibility, this is projected to generate an additional €15–€25 million annually, bringing total system revenue to approximately €305 million per year.

Will there be advertising on the cars?
Yes. Similar to Dublin Bus and Dublin Bikes, TRASNA will generate commercial revenue through vehicle exterior sponsorship and non-intrusive in-app advertising. Given the fleet size and visibility, this is projected to generate an additional €15–€25 million annually, bringing total system revenue to over €300 million per year.
How is the capital cost financed?
Three options: (1) direct Exchequer funding; (2) a 20-year local authority bond at 3.5% interest; or (3) a public-private partnership with the State retaining majority ownership. All three are financially viable and deliver better value than MetroLink.
What is the best-case scenario? What if everyone wants to use it?
If TRASNA becomes as ubiquitous as the smartphone—reaching 80% take-up across Dublin—the county transforms completely. At 80% take-up, the system would require a fleet of approximately 123,000 vehicles to meet peak demand. This would cost €2.96 billion to build (still a fraction of MetroLink). It would generate €681 million in annual revenue, require a €772 million annual subvention, and—crucially—it would remove an estimated 398,000 private cars from Dublin's roads. At that scale, Dublin becomes the world's first post-car county.
Will TRASNA vehicles be allowed to use bus lanes?
Yes. As a licensed public transport service, TRASNA vehicles will have full access to Dublin's 220km network of bus lanes, just as taxis do today. This is a game-changer for journey times. During rush hour, a TRASNA vehicle using the bus lane on the N11 or Drumcondra Road will bypass general traffic, cutting average journey times by up to 30%. Because the vehicles move faster, they complete more trips per hour, which actually reduces the total number of vehicles needed to serve the city. To ensure buses are not delayed, the central AI will dynamically route TRASNA vehicles away from specific bus corridors if they approach capacity.
What happens during a major event, like an All-Ireland Final at Croke Park?
Croke Park holds 82,300 people, creating a massive, localised demand spike. TRASNA handles this through predictive pre-positioning and geofenced pickup zones. The system knows the match schedule and moves thousands of idle vehicles to the surrounding areas (Drumcondra, Ballybough) before the final whistle. Passengers are directed to designated pickup hubs to ensure smooth boarding. While the 3-minute guarantee applies to normal daily operations, wait times immediately outside the stadium after a major match may be 8–12 minutes—still vastly superior to current taxi queues. TRASNA also works in tandem with the DART and Luas, providing the "last mile" connection from the station to your door.
Why not just let Uber or Waymo run it?
Because transport is a public good, not a profit centre. Public ownership means fares stay low, coverage is universal, and profits are reinvested in the system rather than extracted by shareholders. Dublin Bus and Luas are publicly owned for the same reason.
